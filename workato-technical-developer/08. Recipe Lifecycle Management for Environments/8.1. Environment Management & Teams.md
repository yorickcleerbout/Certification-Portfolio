## 🌍 **Environment strategy**

Environment Strategy is about **planning and structuring different environments** so multiple teams can collaborate efficiently. A separate production environment, for example, lets project teams implement new automation without causing operational issues.

> 📌 Beyond production stability, separating dev/test/prod brings four concrete benefits:
> 
> - **🛡️ Security and compliance** — limit access to critical business systems.
> - **👥 Team efficiency** — seamless handover between developers, testers, and operations teams.
> - **🎓 Training and experimentation** — try new platform features and train new team members in a production-like environment without risk.
> - **🏢 Production stability** — untested changes can't accidentally break live automations.

**Planning considerations:** identify recipe lifecycle stages and how many teams you need to support. Document the criteria for moving recipes between stages — e.g. what tests must pass before promotion to production? Many organizations add a **User Acceptance Testing (UAT)** or **staging** environment between test and prod.

---

## 🛡️ **Governance approaches**

Two governance models exist for organizing teams across environments — pick based on what you want to optimize.

---

### 🏗️ Decentralized governance

Give each business unit/group a **distinct, dedicated environment per lifecycle stage**. _Example:_ sales and finance teams each get their own dev, test, and prod environments.

|✅ Pros|❌ Cons|
|---|---|
|Strong separation of concerns|Duplication of effort for common recipes|
|Autonomous environments enable tighter security per area|Hard to enforce best practices and standards across the org|

---

### 🏛️ Centralized governance

**Segment one or more team accounts** so different teams have access to different folders within a shared environment. Sales and finance teams use separate top-level folders (Projects) to segregate access.

|✅ Pros|❌ Cons|
|---|---|
|Better governance and standard enforcement org-wide|Requires fine-grained role management for security|
|Easier audit — identify all users with access to a given resource|All teams share the environment infrastructure|

> 📌 **Two ways to limit team access** in Workato: **separate workspaces per team** (decentralized) OR **different top-level folders in one workspace** (centralized).

---

## 👥 **Teams and roles**

Once governance is decided, **secure the environments** by aligning team members to roles that match what they should be able to do.

---

### 🎯 Key roles and responsibilities

Start by identifying team roles and their responsibilities across lifecycle stages. Typical patterns:

- **🏗️ Change owner** — end-to-end implementation ownership.
- **🚨 Incident manager** — operates recipes in the production environment.
- **✅ Change approver** _(optional)_ — reviews recipes before promotion to higher environments.
- **🚢 Release manager** _(optional)_ — for better-governed recipe promotion.

You can have many team members performing different roles, or the same team member performing additional duties across environments.

---

### ⚙️ System roles vs custom roles

> 📌 Every Workato team account comes with **three pre-defined system roles**: **Admin**, **Analyst**, and **Operator**.

A platform admin assigns one of these to team members depending on their responsibilities. For **fine-grained access**, create **custom roles** using Workato's role-based access control (RBAC).

> 💡 _Example: HR team custom roles_
> 
> - **HR Analysts** — create/update recipes in dev only; restricted from connections and prod access; HR folder only.
> - **HR Operations** — coordinates the recipe promotion process for release control.
> - **HR Manager** — view job history in prod, manage incidents; restricted from creating/updating recipes or connections.

To add team roles, you need to be a team Owner, Admin, or have permission to access the Teams page.

---

### ✅ Teams best practices

A streamlined onboarding process avoids friction for new team members:

- **🏦 Centralize access provisioning** — multiple people creating roles and folders leads to inconsistencies. Centralize with review and approvals (e.g. Workbot-driven request flow with Slack approvals).
- **🔐 Use Single Sign-On (SSO) + Just-in-Time provisioning** — eliminates manual user account setup and permission selection.
- **👀 Monitor and audit access regularly** — good governance is also about _detecting_ violations, not just preventing them. Routinely check the collaborator list.

- What two technologies help eliminate manual user account setup? (Single Sign-On; Just-in-Time provisioning)
- Why audit access regularly even with role-based controls? (To detect violations and prevent unauthorized activity — governance has both a prevent and detect side.)

## 📁 **Organizing environments**

How you organize recipes and name assets matters for both **manageability** and **security**.

---

### 🗂️ Folder strategy

> 📌 **Folders (Projects)** are the unit Workato uses for **import/export between environments**. Organize them well.

Best practices:

- **🏢 One folder per business unit / functional area** — Finance, Sales, HR, etc.
- **🪺 Use nested folders** within functional areas for project-specific organization.
- **🚫 Avoid stuffing too many recipes into a single folder** — especially the default **Home folder**.
- **🔄 Keep the same folder structure and names across dev/test/prod** for uniformity (not required by Workato, but consistent).
- **🏗️ Top-level folders** for shared assets — application connections and callable recipes used across projects.

> 📌 Folders are also the **unit for assigning custom roles** — visibility and access flow through folder membership. Organizing shared assets in top-level folders allows related assets to be **promoted together**.

---

### 🏷️ Naming conventions

A standard naming convention promotes consistency and shared vocabulary across teams.

> 💡 _Example:_ `HRD 123-01 Create G-Suite Account`
> 
> From the name you can tell: the **HR team** manages it, **Automation #123** identifies the business process, **Recipe #01** identifies this specific recipe within the workflow.

Best practices:

- Maintain an **inventory of recipe IDs** in a shared spreadsheet (or other tool) — claim the next ID when creating a new recipe.
- **Don't reuse IDs** of decommissioned or never-launched recipes.
- > ⚠️ **Don't include environment-specific identifiers** (like `Sandbox` or `QA`) in asset names — promoting between environments can produce unexpected outcomes.
    

---

### 🧠 Quick recall

- Folders are the unit used for `_____` between Workato environments. (Import/export)
- Should you put many recipes in a single folder, especially the Home folder? (No — avoid this.)
- Where should application connections and callable recipes shared across projects live? (In top-level folders.)
- Why avoid `Sandbox` or `QA` in asset names? (Promoting between environments can produce unexpected outcomes.)
- What two pieces of information should a recipe name encode? (Automation number for the business process; recipe number for the specific recipe within the workflow.)

## 🚀 **Module key takeaways**

- **Separate environments (dev/test/prod)** unlock four benefits: security/compliance, team efficiency, training freedom, production stability.
- **Two governance approaches**: decentralized (separate workspaces) or centralized (shared workspace, segregated folders).
- **Three default system roles**: Admin, Analyst, Operator. **Custom roles** for fine-grained access.
- **Centralize access provisioning** with SSO + Just-in-Time provisioning; monitor and audit regularly.
- **Folders** = the unit of import/export AND the unit of access control. Top-level folder per business unit; never stuff Home folder; consistent structure across environments.
- **Naming**: include business-process and recipe IDs; avoid environment-specific identifiers.
